## The Fundamental Question

Before we talk about lines, let's ask the question every corner asks you:

**"What speed do you want to carry... and where?"**

Three points matter in every corner:

1. **Entry** - Speed at turn-in
2. **Apex** - Minimum speed mid-corner
3. **Exit** - Speed when you straighten the wheel

Here's the key insight: **You can't maximize all three.**

Physics forces a trade-off:

- Higher entry speed â†’ Lower apex speed â†’ Lower exit speed
- Lower entry speed â†’ Higher apex speed â†’ Higher exit speed

The racing line you choose determines which trade-off you make.

## The Three Racing Lines

### The Geometric Line (Shortest Path)

**What it is:**
The line that minimizes distance traveled. Early turn-in, early apex, tight radius.

**The Math:**

```
Distance = Shortest
Radius = Smallest
Speed = Lowest (for same grip level)
```

Why lowest speed? Because lateral acceleration is:

**a = vÂ² / r**

Where:

- a = lateral acceleration (how much grip you need)
- v = velocity (speed)
- r = radius (how tight the turn is)

Small radius (tight turn) = need more grip OR go slower.

**When to use:**

- Chicanes where minimizing distance matters more than speed
- Very slow corners where exit speed won't matter (another slow corner follows)
- **Rarely the fastest choice** in most corners

**Why it feels "right":**
Humans instinctively minimize distance. But racing isn't about shortest pathâ€”it's about fastest lap.

---

### The Early Apex Line

**What it is:**
Turn in early, apex before the geometric center of the corner, exit tight.

**The trade-off:**
âœ… Higher entry speed (you're still fast when you turn in)  
âŒ Compromised exit (you run out of track, can't get on throttle early)

**When to use:**

- Corners **followed by another corner** (exit speed doesn't matter as much)
- Defending position in a race (blocks the inside, forces opponent wide)
- When the next straight is short

**When NOT to use:**

- Corners leading onto a straight (exit speed is everything)
- When you need to maximize exit momentum

**The Problem:**
Early apex = you're pointed at the outside wall by mid-corner = can't get on throttle = slow exit = bad.

---

### The Late Apex Line (The Money Line)

**What it is:**
Turn in late, apex **past** the geometric center, maximize track width on exit.

**The trade-off:**
âŒ Sacrifice entry speed (brake earlier, turn in later)  
âœ… Higher apex speed (wider radius)  
âœ… **Much higher exit speed** (can straighten wheel and nail throttle earlier)

**The Math:**
Same formula: **a = vÂ² / r**

But now **r is bigger** (wider radius), so:

- You can carry MORE speed (v) with SAME lateral G (a)
- OR generate LESS lateral G (a) at SAME speed (v)

**Master Lonn's Turn 5 Discovery (Week 03, Winton):**

**Before (tight line):**

- Radius: ~15m (tight)
- Speed: 35.7 m/s
- Lateral G: 1.62G (fighting the car!)

**After (wide arc, 2/3 track width):**

- Radius: ~25m (wider)
- Speed: 39.5 m/s
- Lateral G: 1.22G (smooth!)

**Result:** 3.8 m/s more speed, LESS lateral G. That's the power of a late apex wide line.

**When to use:**

- **Default for most corners**
- Especially corners leading onto straights
- Long, flowing corners (Turn 5 type)
- Anytime exit speed matters

## Corner Types and Line Selection

### Hairpins (180Â° turns)

**Challenge:** Very slow minimum speed, but often lead to straights.

**Optimal line:** LATE apex

- Brake deep, turn in late
- Apex is 2/3 through the corner
- Maximize track width on exit
- Early throttle = higher speed down the next straight

**Example:** Rudskogen Sector 2 hairpins (Week 02)

Master Lonn's 57.5% brake bias allowed:

- Later braking
- Better rotation at apex
- Earlier throttle application
- Result: Faster exit, better lap times

---

### Fast Sweepers (High-speed, constant radius)

**Challenge:** Carrying maximum speed while maintaining smooth arc.

**Optimal line:** Geometric to LATE apex hybrid

- Use full track width entry
- Smooth, progressive turn-in
- Apex at or slightly past center
- Use all track on exit
- **Minimize steering input** (smooth = fast)

**Key insight:** At high speed, small steering corrections cost BIG speed.

Master Lonn's data shows:

- Smooth steering = faster corner speed
- Jerky inputs = lateral G spikes = speed loss

---

### Chicanes (Linked corners)

**Challenge:** Multiple direction changes, one corner affects the next.

**Strategy:** Work backwards!

1. Identify the **exit** corner (usually leads to a straight)
2. **That corner gets the late apex** (maximize exit speed)
3. Earlier corners become "throw-away" corners
4. Accept slower entry to earlier corners to set up the exit

**Example Structure:**

```
Corner A (left) â†’ Corner B (right) â†’ Straight

Strategy:
- Corner A: Early apex (tighter, "throw away")
- Corner B: LATE apex (maximize exit onto straight)
```

**Why this works:**
Time gained on the straight > time lost in Corner A.

## Advanced Concepts

### Vision and Reference Points

**The problem:** Looking at the apex means you're always reacting late.

**The solution:** Look where you WANT to go, not where you ARE.

**The practice:**

1. Entry: Look at apex
2. At apex: Look at exit
3. At exit: Already looking at next corner entry

Your hands follow your eyes. Look ahead, the line becomes automatic.

---

### Track Position and Line Adjustment

**Not all tracks are smooth.**

- Bumps â†’ Avoid during heavy braking/turning
- Camber (banking) â†’ Use it! More grip available
- Off-camber â†’ Reduce speed, be gentle
- Elevation change â†’ Affects weight transfer

**Example:** Winton Turn 3 has bumps on entry
Master Lonn's approach: Brake early, get car straight before hitting bumps.

### The "Straightest Line" Principle

At any given speed, the fastest path minimizes time, not distance.

**Key insight:**
Making the car go **straight** as much as possible = faster lap.

Why?

- Longitudinal acceleration (straight line) > lateral acceleration (turning)
- Engine provides forward force (throttle)
- No engine provides sideways force (you just... turn)

Therefore:
Maximize time going straight â†’ Maximize time on throttle â†’ Faster lap.

**How to apply:**
Late apex line creates longest possible "straight" section on exit.

## Padawan Practice Drills

### Drill 1: The Reference Point Challenge

**Goal:** Build muscle memory for late apex.

**Setup:**

- Pick ONE corner at your current track
- Choose a corner that leads to a straight
- Identify your current apex point (where you apex now)

**Drill:**

1. Lap 1-3: Current line (baseline)
2. Lap 4-6: Turn in 2 car-lengths later
3. Lap 7-9: Turn in 4 car-lengths later
4. Lap 10-12: Find optimal (test 2, 3, 4 car-length delay)

**Measure:**

- Exit speed (highest = best line)
- Time to next brake zone
- Lap time through next sector

**Success criteria:**
Found the turn-in point that gives highest exit speed.

---

### Drill 2: The "Use All Track" Drill

**Goal:** Break the habit of staying tight.

**Setup:**

- Practice session, any track
- Focus on ONE corner per session

**Drill:**

1. **Lap 1-5:** Drive normal line
2. **Lap 6-10:** FORCE yourself to use full track width on exit
   - If you're not touching outside rumble strip, you didn't use all track
3. **Compare data:** Which was faster?

**Common finding:**
Using all track = faster exit speed (even if entry feels "wrong").

**Why it works:**
Wider radius = physics wins.

---

### Drill 3: The Chicane Strategy Drill

**Goal:** Learn to sacrifice one corner for the next.

**Setup:**

- Find a chicane (linked left-right or right-left)
- Session focus: Exit the SECOND corner faster

**Drill:**

1. **Approach 1 (equal treatment):** Treat both corners equally
2. **Approach 2 (throw-away first):** Tighter first corner, wider second corner
3. **Approach 3 (throw-away second):** Wider first corner, tighter second corner

**Measure:**

- Speed at exit of second corner
- Lap time through next section

**Expected result:**
Approach 2 (throw-away first corner) usually fastest.
